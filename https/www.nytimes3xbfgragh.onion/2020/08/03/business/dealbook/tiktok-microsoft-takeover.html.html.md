## That escalated quickly

At the end of last week, it seemed that TikTok‚Äôs days in the U.S. were
numbered. ‚ÄúAs far as TikTok is concerned, we‚Äôre banning them from the
United States,‚Äù President Trump said on Friday of the hugely popular
social network, which his administration considers a national security
risk because of its Chinese ownership.

But behind the scenes, Microsoft was exploring a takeover bid for
TikTok‚Äôs American operations, which gained traction thanks to lobbying
from some of the president‚Äôs top advisers and Republican lawmakers.

**Satya Nadella and Mr. Trump spoke on Sunday**, and it appears that the
phone conversation bought the Microsoft chief time to flesh out [the
potential
acquisition](https://www.nytimes3xbfgragh.onion/2020/08/02/business/economy/trump-tiktok-china-national-security.html),
The Times‚Äôs Mike Isaac, Ana Swanson and Maggie Haberman report. The
president had dismissed rumors of Microsoft‚Äôs interest in a deal as a
way to avoid shutting down TikTok, saying, ‚ÄúWe are not an M\&A country.‚Äù

</div>

</div>

<div class="css-1fanzo5 StoryBodyCompanionColumn">

<div class="css-53u6y8">

**Now, Microsoft has six weeks to seal a deal** for TikTok, which says
it has 100 million users in the U.S. In a
[statement](https://blogs.microsoft.com/blog/2020/08/02/microsoft-to-continue-discussions-on-potential-tiktok-purchase-in-the-united-states/),
Microsoft said it would ‚Äúmove quickly‚Äù to pursue a deal with TikTok‚Äôs
parent, Beijing-based ByteDance, aiming to wrap it up by Sept. 15.
Microsoft will conduct ‚Äúa complete security review‚Äù of the company, it
said, and pledged to keep all American users‚Äô data within the U.S. and
sever any connections to systems abroad.

‚Ä¢ Over the weekend, top White House advisers like Treasury Secretary
Steven Mnuchin and Larry Kudlow, the chief of the National Economic
Council, argued the merits of a purchase by Microsoft to Mr. Trump, and
enlisted lawmakers like the Republican Senators John Cornyn, Lindsey
Graham and Marco Rubio to weigh in. The risk of angering TikTok‚Äôs young
user base with a shutdown in the run-up to the election [was another
consideration](https://www.nytimes3xbfgragh.onion/2020/08/02/style/tiktok-ban-threat-trump.html).

‚Ä¢ On the other side, Peter Navarro, the White House‚Äôs top trade adviser,
pushed for a TikTok ban as a means to punish China in the escalating
[tech cold
war](https://www.nytimes3xbfgragh.onion/2020/07/24/business/dealbook/china-us-rivalry.html)
between Washington and Beijing.

**How much will it cost?** Last week, a group of investors, including
Sequoia Capital and General Atlantic, floated a $50 billion bid for
TikTok, [according to
Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/article/us-bytedance-tiktok-exclusive/exclusive-bytedance-investors-value-tiktok-at-50-billion-in-takeover-bid-sources-idUSKCN24U1M9).
Microsoft is interested only in TikTok‚Äôs businesses in the U.S.,
Australia, Canada and New Zealand, and may invite other ‚ÄúAmerican
investors‚Äù to take minority stakes.

‚Ä¢ The time pressure, and alternative of a shut down, may give Microsoft
the upper hand in negotiations with ByteDance. But the Chinese company
does not seem resigned to a forced sale: In [a letter to
employees](https://www.bloombergquint.com/business/bytedance-ceo-letter-working-overtime-on-u-s-tiktok-solution)
today, ByteDance‚Äôs C.E.O., Zhang Yiming, said he disagreed with the
order to sell its American operations and stressed that no decisions
about TikTok‚Äôs fate had been made. ‚ÄúThe attention of the outside world
and rumors around TikTok might last for a while,‚Äù he wrote.

</div>

<div class="css-1fanzo5 StoryBodyCompanionColumn">

<div class="css-53u6y8">

‚Ä¢ What is certain is that TikTok‚Äôs valuation is many times higher than
the $1 billion it cost in 2017 to buy its predecessor, Musical.ly. At
the end of June, Microsoft had just over $136 billion in cash.

**What‚Äôs in it for Microsoft?** ‚ÄúThe fickle infatuation of tweens,‚Äù The
Times‚Äôs Karen Weise
[writes](https://www.nytimes3xbfgragh.onion/2020/08/03/technology/tiktok-microsoft-tweens.html).
More seriously, taking over TikTok would give the tech giant ‚Äúcontrol of
one of the largest and most influential social networks in the country.‚Äù
If a deal gets done, it could go a few different ways:

‚Ä¢ A transformative Microsoft-TikTok tie up could create meaningful
competition for Facebook and Google, leveraging other Microsoft brands
like LinkedIn, Minecraft and Xbox. But at a time when Big Tech is under
attack for its market dominance, it is surprising to see the White House
push TikTok ‚Äî inadvertently or not ‚Äî into the arms of one of the largest
companies in the world. Slack recently accused Microsoft of
[anticompetitive
behavior](https://www.nytimes3xbfgragh.onion/2020/07/22/technology/slack-microsoft-antitrust.html).

‚Ä¢ Microsoft‚Äôs experience with consumer businesses is mixed, so the
successful stewardship of TikTok is not assured ‚Äî think of the search
engine Bing or the ill-fated takeover of Nokia‚Äôs smartphone business.
And in June, Microsoft said it was shutting down Mixer, a
video-game-streaming platform, and closing all of its retail stores.

‚Ä¢ The biggest risk, perhaps, is for the rest of Microsoft. Will the
company‚Äôs management need to devote so much attention to TikTok and its
attendant political issues that it distracts from its [very
profitable](https://www.cnbc.com/2020/07/22/microsoft-msft-earnings-q4-2020.html)
core of serving business customers?

## Here‚Äôs what is happening

## Venture fund-raising is alive and (relatively) well

The coronavirus has made it more difficult for entrepreneurs to raise
money. But for many companies ‚Äî from established businesses to smaller
start-ups ‚Äî the pandemic hasn‚Äôt prevented them from finding investors
willing to open their wallets.

</div>

<div class="css-53u6y8">

**TransferWise recently raised $319 million in a secondary deal,** in
which existing shareholders will sell some of their holdings in the
European fintech company. [The
transaction](https://techcrunch.com/2020/07/28/transferwise-five-unicorns/)
values TransferWise at about $5 billion, a [nearly 43 percent
bump](https://www.forbes.com/sites/samshead/2019/05/22/transferwise-valued-at-35-billion-as-founders-sell-stake/)
from last year. Kristo Kaarmann, the company‚Äôs C.E.O., told Michael that
TransferWise, which was founded in 2011, didn‚Äôt *need* to raise money,
since it has been profitable for three years now. But it saw an
opportunity for older investors and long-tenured employees to sell some
of their stakes.

**Smaller companies aren‚Äôt afraid to embark on fund-raising either**,
even if the pandemic has changed the way they court potential investors.

‚Ä¢
[Ro](https://www.cnbc.com/2020/07/27/ro-raises-200-million-at-1point5-billion-valuation-250-million-sales.html),
a telehealth start-up, said last week that it had raised $200 million in
new funding.

‚Ä¢ [Gro Intelligence](https://gro-intelligence.com/), which uses
artificial intelligence to gather agricultural data, is in the process
of raising an ‚Äúopportunistic‚Äù Series B round with a target of $50
million, its founder and C.E.O., Sara Menker, told Michael.

‚Ä¢ [Shield AI](http://shield.ai/), which makes A.I. software and
autonomous drones for the military and law enforcement, recently kicked
off its latest round of fund-raising.

**It has taken time for investors to get comfortable, well, investing.**
Venture deals in the second quarter in the U.S., Europe and Asia were
all down from the same time last year, but up from the previous quarter,
[according to PwC and CB
Insights](https://t.yesware.com/tt/e001b3127892bff3127c5684106b4fface2be613/6d096e53b8a44a013ebee1131ad0c628/67276689dfad2ab3eddda9aeeb223346/www.cbinsights.com/research/report/venture-capital-q2-2020/).
‚ÄúThere‚Äôs obviously a lot more gun-shyness,‚Äù Ms. Menker of Gro
Intelligence said, with many venture capitalists initially focused on
protecting existing portfolio companies rather than striking new
