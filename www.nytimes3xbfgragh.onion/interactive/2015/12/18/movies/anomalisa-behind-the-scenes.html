    Showing the Seams in ‘Anomalisa’

    By MEKADO MURPHY DEC. 18, 2015

    The directors Charlie Kaufman and Duke Johnson discuss the difficult work of bringing puppets to life in their stop-motion film. Related Article

    In the stop-motion animated film “Anomalisa,” the ordinary and familiar take on imaginative, out-of-the-ordinary shapes. Directed by Charlie Kaufman and Duke Johnson, the movie follows the writer and motivational speaker Michael Stone (voiced by David Thewlis) on a routine business trip that becomes energized when he meets one particular woman, Lisa (voiced by Jennifer Jason Leigh).

    The film mainly takes place in the hotel where Michael is staying, a mundane setting that isn’t the whimsical kind of place audiences might expect from stop-motion. Its adult subject matter and themes are also uncommon for the format. But Mr. Johnson, who had been directing offbeat animated work like the series “Mary Shelley’s Frankenhole” through the company Starburns Industries, saw visual potential in Mr. Kaufman’s story, which began as a “sound play,” in which actors read their roles accompanied by sound effects, in 2005.

    “We wanted to take the medium of stop-motion animation into realms that we hadn’t seen it go before,” Mr. Johnson said, “more adult-themed, emotionally authentic, nuanced material.”

    In a recent interview, Mr. Johnson and Mr. Kaufman discussed some of the ideas behind the film and how they came to develop its visual aesthetic. Here is a look at some of those elements, with commentary from the filmmakers.

    1. The Puppets

      The puppets, which have a hauntingly authentic quality, were based on people the filmmakers photographed. A sculptor then used those images to mold a basic model.

      The filmmakers designed the puppets to match the intent of their actions, subtle features to go with tempered performances. To create the faces, they used a 3-D printing technique — first made popular in stop-motion features by the studio Laika — where expressions are printed and replaced on the puppets. The faces are broken at eye level, leaving a seam.

      Usually those seams are digitally removed to make a more realistic face, but here Mr. Kaufman and Mr. Johnson made those seams part of the aesthetic of the characters, to reflect their fragile, somewhat broken state.

      “We didn’t want to hide the fact that it’s stop-motion,” Mr. Kaufman said. “We didn’t want to paint out the thing that it was. When the animators touch the clothing to move it, you can sometimes see clothing moving. It’s often cleaned up in post, but we wanted that feeling of the unseen presence of the animators.”

    2. The Sex Scene

      An intimate encounter between Michael and Lisa was a challenge narratively and technically.

      “We wanted this scene, like every other scene in the movie, to play emotionally true,” Mr. Kaufman said. “We knew that there was the expectation that puppet sex would be a joke and has been played that way before. We didn’t want it to be played that way. We didn’t want to betray the characters at that point.”

      It took six months to shoot the sex scene. Challenges arose in how to animate puppets removing their clothes, as their bodies don’t bend like a human body does. “You have to cut them and break them and rip them and put them back together,” Mr. Johnson said. Considerations had to be made about how the fabric looked as it was taken off. Figuring out how to have puppet bodies, made of silicon, meld with each other required advanced engineering.

      “It was the most complicated scene in this movie,” Mr. Johnson said, “and in anything any of the animators have ever done.”

    3. The Hotel Room

      Key cards, bad artwork and muted colors are frequently a part of the hotel experience. “Anomalisa” has a way of getting that familiar space right in a miniature way. The filmmakers paid attention to detail for the room (the set can be seen in an exhibition about the film at the Museum of the Moving Image in Queens through March).

      “It’s like everywhere and nowhere,” Mr. Kaufman said. “When the bellhop opens the door to that room, we wanted it to be a room that almost everybody has stayed in. We designed a room that looks like that, one that feels specific, but generic.”

      From the lamps to the desk chair to even the thermostat, the filmmakers and animators were meticulous about creating the ambience of the hotel. “There’s something cool about a really accurate miniature version of a hotel to me,” Mr. Kaufman said. They enhanced those details with color and lighting to reflect Michael’s emotional experience.
