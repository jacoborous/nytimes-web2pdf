Like the New ‘Superfly’? Stream These 13 Classic Blaxploitation Films

by Richard Newby

June 18, 2018

Trevor Jackson, left, and Jason Mitchell in “Superfly.”Bob Mahoney/Columbia Pictures

In the heat of summer, there’s nothing cooler than Blaxploitation films. From streetwise hustlers to tough men of action to foxy lady vigilantes with the weight of the world on their shoulders, the heroes of Blaxploitation films brought a new and unapologetically black perspective to American cinema in the ’70s, opening it to audiences that were hungry to see movies about people like themselves, with struggles like theirs — and perhaps enjoy a bit of vicarious wish-fulfillment as well. And as the buzz surrounding last week’s release of Director X’s “Superfly” remake attests, Blaxploitation’s traversal of genre, class and social norms left a lasting influence on the political and aesthetic sensibilities of generations of filmmakers and fans.

If you haven’t yet delved into the world of Blaxploitation, or you’re simply looking for more, the streaming service Brown Sugar has an expansive collection of Blaxploitation films that range from DVD quality to grainy transfers that recall an era of drive-ins and corner cinemas. Brown Sugar is available for $3.99 a month with the option to watch on the website or subscribe using the app, which you can download to your mobile device or your streaming device, including Roku, Apple TV and Amazon Fire Stick. Brown Sugar is also available as an add-on channel for Amazon Prime Video subscribers. (See Brown Sugar’s F.A.Q. page for more details.)

Brown Sugar features a wide range of movies from multiple categories, including action, drama, comedy, horror and romance. Not all are Blaxploitation films, but the variety of those that are underscores the notion that Blaxploitation was always more of a movement than a genre. Given Brown Sugar’s ever-growing collection, it’s hard to know just where to dive in. Here are a few suggestions on some of the best Blaxploitation films the service has to offer.

A scene from “Superfly.”Warner Bros.

‘Superfly’ (1972)

An obvious place to start after watching the remake, “Superfly” incorporates an stylish fashion sense and memorable soundtrack by Curtis Mayfield to bring a sense of class and soul to the world of drug dealers in an early example of Ghetto Fabulous. Directed by Gordon Parks Jr. (whose father found success a year earlier with “Shaft”), “Superfly” trades cops for crooks and still manages to find a moral center. Youngblood Priest (Ron O’Neal), dressed to the nines and adorned with a cross necklace that doubles as a coke spoon, wants to escape his life of drug dealing and go straight. He needs to complete one last job before walking away, but the job leads him deep into a conspiracy, one that costs more than he counted on.

Richard Roundtree in “Shaft.”MGM, via Everett Collection

‘Shaft’ (1971)

Arguably the most iconic of Blaxploitation films, Gordon Parks’s “Shaft” went a long way in terms of inducting Blaxploitation into the popular culture of its time. Detective John Shaft (Richard Roundtree) acts with action hero efficiency to take down a New York mob, but his presence demands respect not only from black militants but from a number of white members of the police force as well. “Shaft” served as a bridge between black and white audiences, a fact that was rewarded with both a Grammy and an Academy Award for Isaac Hayes’s soundtrack. The film spawned two sequels, the first of which, “Shaft’s Big Score,” is also available on Brown Sugar. Expect the character to return in a major way next year when “Son of Shaft” unites three generations. Can you dig it?

Pam Grier in “Coffy.”MGM

‘Coffy’ (1973)

A year before Charles Bronson took his revenge to the streets in “Death Wish,” Pam Grier was dealing out vigilante justice on her own terms. This influential film by Jack Hill follows a nurse who seeks revenge on her entire community’s drug organization. Disguising herself as a prostitute, Coffy takes down the pushers in a variety of extreme ways, the most notable of which involves the metal shiv she hides in her afro. Considered a Blaxploitation classic today, “Coffy” wasn’t met with enough success in its time to engender any sequels, but Grier and Hill rejoined the next year for the similarly themed “Foxy Brown.” Both of these films served as the inspiration for Quentin Tarantino’s “Jackie Brown,” which helped put the spotlight back on Grier.

A scene from “Sweet Sweetback’s Baad Asssss Song.”Yeah, via Everett Collection

‘Sweet Sweetback’s Baadasssss Song’ (1971)

It’s rough, it’s dirty, and there’s nothing sweet about it. “Sweetback” gave birth to the Blaxploitation era. Directed, written, produced, composed, edited and starring Melvin Van Peebles, “Sweetback” is notable for its unflinching look at urban black neighborhoods. The film follows a young man, gifted with extraordinary sexual prowess, as he navigates South Central Los Angeles and is blamed for a murder he didn’t commit. Chased by the police and the Hell’s Angels, he travels through a world where there is no safety or quarter for a black man. While neither the most enjoyable nor most competently made film on this list, it’s a must watch for film-history buffs looking for this cinema movement’s origins.

Pam Grier in “Friday Foster.”American International Pictures, via Everett Collection

‘Friday Foster’ (1975)

It’s a racially-charged conspiracy film given all the charm of Howard Hawks’s “His Girl Friday.” Based on the comic strip of the same name, “Friday Foster” follows a former-model-turned-photojournalist (played by the Blaxploitation queen Pam Grier) who uncovers an assassination plot against the nation’s wealthiest African-American. Although less action-packed than other films featuring Grier, its political nature feels particularly relevant because of its concerns over white supremacists in high places. Despite the twists and turns the film takes, the high point is the comedic banter between Grier and Yaphet Kotto. And with Eartha Kitt playing the role of a fashion designer who figures into the conspiracy, “Friday Foster” is more fun than it has any right to be.

A scene from “Detroit 9000.”Rolling Thunder Films

‘Detroit 9000’ (1973)

This combination of a buddy-cop picture and a heist flick is a favorite of Quentin Tarantino’s, and it’s the easiest to recommend to anyone looking for some flair and blockbuster panache. Directed by Arthur Marks (“Friday Foster”), “Detroit 9000” once again blends political intrigue with humor, but it takes a darker turn. The film puts together a young black detective and an old-school white detective to solve a case of stolen funds that could swing a campaign against a black congressman. Dealing with the racial divide of Detroit, the film explores a city that is tearing itself apart. With a bloody shootout to rival any of its contemporaries, and a moral dilemma that offers no easy answers, “Detroit 9000” challenges notions of justice as frequently as it excites with its buddy dynamics and high-action set pieces.

Isaac Hayes in “Truck Turner.”American International Pictures

‘Truck Turner’ (1974)

Isaac Hayes’s Blaxploitation legacy may be most often identified with “Shaft,” but “Truck Turner” is one of the greatest things that soul man ever put forth. The former football player, Truck Turner (Isaac Hayes), now a bounty hunter, finds himself up against a ruthless Madame, played by Star Trek’s Nichelle Nichols, and a sociopathic pimp played by Yaphet Kotto. Devoid of any larger conspiracies or social lessons, “Truck Turner” keeps the stakes real but is wholly interested in providing an action-packed and good-humored time — and in doing it with a wink and a smile. One of Blaxploitation’s strongest hidden gems, and a recent addition to Brown Sugar’s library, “Truck Turner” is the perfect entry for a movie night with friends.

William Marshall in “Blacula.”American International Pictures, via Everett Collection

‘Blacula’ (1972)

In its ability to cross genres, Blaxploitation made no exception for horror. Sure, “Blacula” has become a bit of a joke over the course of the decades since its release, and it isn’t exactly terrifying, but it has enough blood and gothic sensibility to deliver some genuinely creepy moments. One of many Blaxploitation horror movies, “Blacula” is often lumped with “Blackenstein” and “Dr. Black and Mr. Hyde,” which are also available on Brown Sugar. Offering gothic romance with an African twist, “Blacula” follows Prince Mamuwalde (William Marshall), a vampire whose new neighborhood rejects him at every turn, as he falls in love with a girl. A poignant tragedy, “Blacula” takes itself far more seriously than its title and reputation would suggest. It’s sequel, “Scream Blacula Scream,” is also available on Brown Sugar.

Tamara Dobson in “Cleopatra Jones.”Warner Bros., via Everett Collection

‘Cleopatra Jones’ (1973)

Bad women doing it for themselves didn’t stop with Pam Grier: Tamara Dobson carved out her own territory as Cleopatra Jones. Tapping into the popularity of the James Bond films, “Cleopatra Jones” put the Bond girl type front and center as Jones, a federal agent, went after deranged drug lord known as Mommy (Shelley Winters). Equipped with a car full of gadgets, a background in martial arts and a love for her people, Cleopatra Jones worked for the United States government, which made her stand apart from her fellow Blaxploitation heroes. While it offers more flair than grit, fans of the espionage genre will get a kick out of this film’s stylish twist on the familiar conventions. The film’s sequel, “Cleopatra Jones and the Casino of Gold,” is also available on Brown Sugar.

Jim Brown in “Slaughter.”Quad Cinema

‘Slaughter’ (1972)

Ten years before Rambo drew first blood, there was the one-man army “Slaughter.” Taking a step out of the neighborhood, the film follows Vietnam veteran Slaughter, played by the human wrecking ball and former Cleveland Browns running back Jim Brown as he hunts down the mobsters who killed his father. Slaughter’s explosive crusade led to his teaming up with United States federal agents in South America in order to destroy a supercomputer used to run a criminal empire. It’s full-blooded nonsense that serves as a likely, partial inspiration to the Blaxploiation satire “Black Dynamite.” “Slaughter” lacked the social justice aspect of many of its counterparts, but it made Brown one of the most prominent actors of the Blaxploitation era. “Black Gunn,” in which Brown also stars, is available on Brown Sugar as well.

John Daniels in “The Candy Tangerine Man.” -Moonstone Entertainment

‘The Candy Tangerine Man’ (1975)

With its biting jive talk, violent extremes and unabashed preoccupation with sex, this film may be too vulgar for some. But despite its crude makeup, “The Candy Tangerine Man” follows a pimp know as the Black Baron (John Daniels), who rules the streets by night and by day resumes his life as a hard-working suburban father and husband. While the film’s ethics are questionable at best, it is an interesting examination of the kind of superheroism attributed to pimps and hustlers. Despite being a later entry in the Blaxploitation canon, this film operates with a seediness that feels closer to “Sweetback” than some of the more commercially appealing Blaxploitation films that came later.

Sheila Frazier and Jim Brown in “Three the Hard Way.”Allied Artists Pictures

‘Three The Hard Way’ (1974)

Bringing together the stars Jim Brown, Fred Williamson and Jim Kelly, this feature from Gordon Parks Jr. is the “Avengers” of Blaxploitation films. Together, gifted with the skills of sharpshooting, martial arts and all around charm, these three heroes take on a neo-Nazi brigade that plans to commit genocide against African-Americans by poisoning the water supply with a newly discovered formula. While the film is far more interested in explosions than in plot, it is well-shot and delivers some noteworthy across-state action. The chemistry among the three leads, who all seem to share the screen amiably, makes this a Blaxploitation event film. In addition to several films starring Brown, plenty of films starring Williamson and Kelly can be found in Brown Sugar’s library as well.

A scene from “Coonskin.”Xenon Pictures

‘Coonskin’ (1975)

In “Coonskin,” Blaxploitation gets animated by the hand of the famed director Ralph Bakshi. Controversial upon release, the film has become a tough-to-find cult classic that reimagines Disney’s “Song of the South.” Brother Rabbit, Brother Bear and Preacher Fox are forced to leave their home in the south and move to Harlem, where they get mixed up in a world of organized crime, minstrel show satire and white supremacists. Featuring the voice work of Barry White and Scatman Crothers, the film takes an iconoclastic look at American history and the role of black people within it. “Coonskin” offers a broad pastiche of ’70s filmmaking conventions and possess a wicked sense of humor, making it a Blaxploitation film unlike any other.
